Cryptocurrency Fraud and Crime

For people outside of the technology community, they often hear about cryptocurrency in connection with get-rich-quick schemes, scams, and fraud. These are a real problem which should be explained and addressed when reaching new users.

Why is cryptocurrency so often part of scams?

  • Transactions are irreversible, so once the scammer has access to the account or money, it's over, unless you have resources to identify the scammer.
  • Unlike most eCommerce sites, hackers only need to succeed for a few minutes to irreversibly steal cryptocurrency. This means hackers are highly motivated to break into an exchange or popular social network (as in 2020's Twitter CryptoForHealth scam). The best option for exchanges is to keep their money at rest in secure cold wallets.
  • People are unfamiliar with how cryptocurrency *normally* works, beyond that some people made money from it, so they may trust a scammer and their explanations.
  • Small groups can use early investments to rapidly expand the scam, mimicking a global company or MLM scheme, sponsoring events, and hiring employees, without facing due diligence from banks, investors, or journalists. The BBC podcast, The Missing Cryptoqueen, covers a massive scam.
  • Bitcoin transactions leave a record, but criminals can work from countries which do not take cyber-crime seriously, and sell the coins without connecting their identity to the sale.
  • New accounts are created instantly, so communities can't fully block their addresses. Some wallets block warn you on known scam domains. Centralized currencies (such as USDC) have blocked addresses, but they collect additional identifying information from users.
  • Younger, less experienced investors are looking for a place to put their money and a glossy startup page (like the SEC's HoweyCoins) uses all of the tricks of internet marketing.

Some investments are just poor investments

A sore point for many cryptocurrency investors are altcoins and ICOs (described in the Definitions section). Investing early is a high-risk, high-reward process. As long as the creators of an altcoin are truthful about their experience, funding, and belief in their idea, foolish confidence or not, a new cryptocurrency is a new startup - not a scam. Serious investors should be honest with themselves - if they don't know the team or understand the technology, why risk it?

In reality, few altcoins and ICOs were popular or profitable for investors in the long run. Many famous projects lost funding and ceased operations within less than a year. Bitcoin has seen huge price swings and losses, but unreliable new projects can lose all of their value.
