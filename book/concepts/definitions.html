All of the Definitions

At the end of some books, you might find a glossary of unfamiliar terms.

In cryptocurrency there are many new terms for a new industry, and some words can often confused by misuse in popular media. You should take time to understand and feel comfortable before diving in.

What is a blockchain?

A cryptocurrency has a ledger of transactions from person to person. When Alice wants to send a message 'I send $10 to Bob' it can be verifiably signed by her, and the community can credit Bob and debit Alice's account, if she holds enough to make the payment.

Valid transactions get bundled together into blocks, which are issued in an ongoing sequence, confirming the timeline of transactions leading up to the current state. If you download the entire blockchain and follow it from beginning to end, you will know how much Bitcoin is currently stored in every account.

As long as people trade Bitcoin, we will need to know the value available in their accounts, so every transaction is recorded permanently in the blockchain. This has inspired creative uses of the blockchain for permanent data, including avoiding censorship, verifying artists, and marriage proposals.

Bitcoin

The original blockchain-based cryptocurrency (combining several older ideas in the cryptography / cypherpunk world). Bitcoin has always held the highest market cap of any cryptocurrency, but others have come close. The amount of Bitcoin available is regulated with algorthmic scarcity; eventually it will max out at 21 million Bitcoin.

Satoshi Nakamoto: Who invented Bitcoin?

As of this writing, it's not known.

The concept of a cryptocurrency emerged in the 1980s when technologists started discussing how the internet could change how information, money, and governmental power were organized. Some of these ideas influenced how we now freely share news and shop online today. Other ideas remain unrealized, particularly an alternative to the banking system.

The Bitcoin whitepaper in 2008 (followed by software release in 2009) approached the problem by combining two ideas - blockchain and Proof of Work (PoW) - in a way which was compelling to cypherpunks. The author used the name 'Satoshi Nakamoto' and left several comments on message boards (now recorded at the Nakamoto Institute).  Researchers have scrutinized these messages, log in times, spelling and word choice, cryptographic signatures, hacked email inboxes, and other minute details without finding who 'Satoshi' might be. There has been no verified Satoshi-authored messages since 2011, as the Bitcoin codebase moved on to new maintainers, and gained notoriety in the press in connection with WikiLeaks and other platforms.

There have been two major 'announcements' of Satoshi's identity: Newsweek identifying Dorian Satoshi Nakamoto in California (denied and since dismissed), and Wired and Gizmodo suggesting Craig Wright (who then did press with The Economist, BBC, and London Review of Books). In follow-ups, Wright has yet to sign a public message with one of Satoshi's keys, and explain inconsistencies in his professional bios. This has made him a controversial figure in the cryptocurrency community.

So again, it's not known.

Satoshi: Theories over the years
  • Many sources (including a New York Times article) speculate that Nick Szabo, an economist with a history of digital currency research, was involved. Satoshi’s original white paper cited several experts in the field but missed Szabo. Others claim that Szabo has edited old blog posts to disguise connections to Bitcoin. He continues to appear at Bitcoin events.
  • The New Yorker considered several cryptography researchers in 2011 and pointed to one, Michael Clear.
  • Others suggested that a wider group of academics launched Bitcoin and registered several domains connected to it before it was announced.
  • Others speculate about when core developers Martti Malmi and Gavin Andresen, or first Bitcoin recipient Hal Finney, joined the project, and whether one of them used 'Satoshi Nakamoto' as a pen name.
  • Paul Graham of Y-Combinator suggested that Bitcoin is made by a government, as it has no public authors or prior published research:
    1. To finance their own black operations.
    2. Because they thought digital currencies were inevitable, and they preferred bitcoin to some potentially more malevolent form. (Could bitcoin have been worse from a government’s point of view?)
    3. A friend suggested this: because they felt their currency would never become the standard reserve currency, and they felt it was better that no one’s be if theirs couldn’t be.
    4. A variant of the above: the US did it because it seemed inevitable that the dollar would eventually lose its place as the standard reserve currency, and better to have it replaced by bitcoin that the yuan.

Ethereum

A cryptocurrency and blockchain which includes additional features for programming  smart contracts. Ethereum was founded by a small team of known programmers, with Vitalik Buterin continuing to run the Ethereum Foundation and promote it on social media and at conferences worldwide.

Other Coins

Less mainstream cryptocurrencies are generally called "altcoins". There are thousands of altcoins which are copies or experimental projects.

Litecoin

A cryptocurrency and blockchain which is closely related to Bitcoin, but uses a different algorithm (scrypt) for mining. This algorithm ideally provides a more level playing field for mining on smaller machines.

The founder Charlie Lee famously (and controversially) has sold all of his Litecoin holdings, to avoid potential conflicts of interest around manipulating or protecting the price.

ZCash

Zcash doesn't have as high of a market cap as other cryptocurrencies, but is interesting for developments of an encrypted blockchain and zk-Snarks (discussed in a future chapter). Unlike other blockchains, where the transaction history is publicly visible, it is not possible to view Zcash history.

Namecoin

A concept to decentralize internet addresses, allowing anyone to register a domain name anonymously. Once addresses are in the blockchain they cannot be censored because it’s distributed on so many computers. More recently a similar concept has been programmed into Ethereum as ENS.

Centralized Coins: Libra and others

Discussions of regulating cryptocurrency reopened after Facebook engineers announced the creation of a new cryptocurrency, Libra, in 2019. Facebook's plans to support Libra globally on their site, WhatsApp, and Instagram have become unclear after after corporate partners left the organization, and discussions were held in US Congress. Mercy Corps announced their support for using Libra to support the unbanked. Michael Kimani countered with an article 5 Reasons Why Facebook’s New Cryptocurrency ‘Libra’ is Bad News for Africa.

There are similar cryptocurrencies being tested by governments or national banks, but I will explore those in more detail in the Stablecoins section.

Why is a cryptocurrency with corporate or government support so difficult to digest? Detractors point to one or multiple of these points:

  • It goes against the Bitcoin ethos of making a currency without central control
  • Big organizations make a new currency because they intend to profit from, track, and control users' wallets
  • Big organizations cannot successfully make a truly open currency because they will face high standards from KYC regulations
  • Companies tend to pitch a new cryptocurrency or token as a last-ditch appeal for funding (Norwegian Air, Kik Messenger)
  • Some cryptocurrency platforms are maintained and held by a small group of people

What are forks?

In the free and open source software communities, programmers have access to the internal source code of programs and the standards for communicating. If the administrator of a popular open source project stops working on the code, or makes an unpopular decision, tech-savvy users may decide to split the code off into a new "fork", and take control of it.

A fork can be motivated by any disagreement (technical, personal, legal, or political), especially when groups lack a solid leadership or decision-making process. Escalating a disagreement to this level is risky - it can divide the community and double technical work (as any bugs now need to be fixed in both projects).

​Due to the open source code, decentralized technologies, and spontaneous communities popular in cryptocurrencies, it is common for a cryptocurrency and its blockchain / history to be forked. Usually after the fork, an account with 1 Coin becomes two accounts: 1 Coin-A and 1 Coin-B. If you have ever owned Bitcoin, it's possible that you have coins in other blockchains without knowing it! If you don't plan to use Coin-B, you could use your private key to access your wallet and sell your holdings there.

After creating a fork, it is difficult to win over a community, especially when a fork may threaten instability in people's savings and investments. Before high-profile forks, miners typically embed a signal showing their intention into their messages, allowing anyone to gauge popularity of a decision. If even 10% of miners indicate a plan to move, this may motivate other miners to take a fork more seriously.

Ethereum Classic

Earlier you learned that Ethereum's blockchain contains programs known as smart contracts. In 2016, an attacker manipulated a loophole in one of these contracts ("the DAO") and siphoned off money which many users had invested. As much as 1/6th of all issued Ether currency had been invested into the DAO.

The lead Ethereum developers decided to intervene and to reverse / "roll back" the outcome of The DAO as though it had not happened. This appalled some members of the community - what future contracts might get reversed or deleted? A fork was created without this change, and the new, non-reversed branch became known as Ethereum Classic. This was the highest-profile cryptocurrency fork of the time, so initially there was confusion from users and apps. For example, a user might create a wallet address on Blockchain-A, and try to send money from their wallet on Blockchain-B. The money would be lost.

Today you can tell them apart on exchanges as Ethereum (ETH) and Ethereum Classic (ETC).

Bitcoin Cash and Bitcoin SV

For years, Bitcoin had considered changes over block size. The ideal block size on the blockchain became a divisive issue, with each side claiming that they would lower transaction fees and improve uptake of Bitcoin worldwide. There were concerns that a larger block size would make it difficult for everyday users to run a full node, or that technical decisions were influenced by Chinese mining organizations and internet connectivity. Bitcoin developer Mike Hearn quit over the dispute. Websites Bitcoin.com and Bitcoin.org linked to opposing sides, Reddit split into two communities: /r/bitcoin and /r/btc, major community Twitter accounts were split as well.

The difficulties in Ethereum's fork raised users' fears and prolonged the debate. Everyone was afraid to weaken the Bitcoin community through technical failures, media speculation, and potential power struggles. A split seemed inevitable, but what would it mean? Could Bitcoin point-of-sale and checkout apps support both currencies? Was there sufficient interest in a new fork? Who would be the REAL Bitcoin?

Ultimately, Bitcoin Cash (BCH) split from the newly-named "Bitcoin Core" (BTC) on August 201?.

In 2018, Bitcoin SV forked again from Bitcoin Cash over disagreements between two members: Craig Wright and Roger Ver.

Ycash

Ycash is a recent (2019) fork of Zcash. Part of the Zcash transactions goes to a founder/developer fee to keep the currency sustainable, which has been controversial. Ycash wrote an open letter announcing plans to move to a new blockchain and avoid these fees.

Transaction fees

Who sets fees, and where do they go?

Cryptocurrency miners combine multiple transactions into blocks. Each transaction adds a fee (based on a prevailing rate suggested by your wallet app, based on current traffic) and this fee is paid toward the miners. After a cryptocurrency with a fixed supply is fully mined, miners will continue operating on the proceeds of these transactions. A fee also discourages spammers and others from polluting the blockchain with tiny microtransactions ('dust').

  • Fees were a major concern when traffic increases on the blockchain - during Bitcoin's peak value in December 2017, transactions cost on average $28. Blockchain changes and Lightning Network have already improved on this.
  • If you have a transaction with zero fee allocated, it's possible that you or a charitable miner could include it in a block, but it likely would sit unconfirmed for a long time or be returninged to you. Without a fee, there is no motivation for any miner to check the validity.
  • By offering a higher-than-normal fee, you can improve your chance of a quickly confirmed transaction.
  • A few transactions have an extraordinary fee and little payment, which may be a method for miners to transfer or launder money, or caused by a hacked exchange.

Addresses and Wallets

Just like you can store cash and credit cards in a physical wallet, a cryptocurrency wallet stores one or more cryptocurrencies in an app on your phone, computer, browser, or physical device.

Early on, people would have one address in their wallet to send and receive money, similar to how you likely have one main email address or phone number. Eventually people became more concerned about how every input and output was public on the blockchain. Suppose that a restaurant uses only one blockchain address - if you use it or even see that address, then you would see their income, times when customers paid, and all of those customers' addresses, and continue collecting information on those customers.

In most current applications, your wallet app manages a practically unlimited number of addresses from a single seed. Instead of printing an address on a paper menu, where it might be mistyped, a customer is shown a screen or a custom address to pay their bill. When you make a payment from an address, your app may create a new "change address" and send the remaining coins there. To an outside observer, it will be unclear what was a payment and what was retained in your wallet.

Cold Wallets

Out of fears that an online wallet may be hacked, some cryptocurrency keys are saved to USB drives stored offline or even in safe-deposit boxes, known as 'cold storage'. To be effective, all of the key information (or all of the key minus the password) should be stored offline and deleted from the originating computer. You should test carefully before sending money to a cold wallet address, to verify that it is easily recoverable and that you've remembered the address and passcode correctly.

​What is a paper wallet?

Some cold wallet users print out the key information (or the key minus a password) onto paper, and store that in a secure location. Do not take photos of your paper wallet! As with any other cold wallet, you should verify that it is easily recoverable and that you've remembered the address and passcode correctly.

​Why can't we use paper wallets as cash?

If I can print an address and key information onto paper, why not print my own money? This idea has been pursued many times, but doesn't pass a rigorous thought process. Suppose that I hand you a paper wallet and promise that the associated address has $10 of Bitcoin.

  • Without internet access, you have no way to verify the current value of the address. You refuse the bill as payment.
  • With internet access you check the blockchain and verify the current value, but I could be copying this bill many times over with the same address/key information. By the time you get home, someone may have transferred the money out, so the address on the bill is always at risk of becoming worthless.
  • If you prevent that loss by immediately moving money from the bill to a different address which you control, the account associated with the bill has lost its value; there is no point to printing a bill which cannot be reused.

What is a brain wallet?

This is a wallet file, paper wallet, or app which combined with the passcode in your memory, can restore access to your wallet and keys. It's important to use a long password/passphrase and an app that you trust.

Exchanges

As mentioned in the Basics section, an exchange is a company or decentralized app which allows trading between several cryptocurrencies and conventional currency. It may have Know Your Customer (KYC) restrictions, or limit who can sign up for accounts.

Smart Contracts

Smart contracts are programs which accompany an address on a blockchain. Bitcoin contained a limited programming interface, which developers experimented with for escrow accounts and other simple financial programs. Ethereum was created with a focus on distributed computing and more feature-rich languages to write smart contracts.

Consider a Payroll contract. The contract is associated with an address where a co-op receives all of its profits. One Manager can add and remove Members and set their share of the profit. Every payday, the contract distributes its money. It's not clear why a co-op chose a smart contract in place of a conventional payroll, but I can think of some potential benefits:

  • profits and salaries are always transparent
  • maybe the Co-Op's customers / clients pay in cryptocurrency
  • maybe the Members want to be paid in cryptocurrency or have no bank account
  • the Manager and Members might have some limits or voting power in the contract
Smart contracts can be coded in different languages which compile to a common bytecode before entering the blockchain. A common JavaScript-based language is Solidity.

Gas

Smart contract platforms add another fee: "gas". Every node in the network has to run contract code to verify its outcome. To discourage users from overusing the network's computation power and storage, and to compensate miners, a gas fee is applied to smart contract transactions. The amount of gas will depend on the needs of your program.

EIPs and ERCs

An EIP (Ethereum Improvement Proposal) is a volunteered proposal to expand the features and functionality of Ethereum. The Ethereum community has a formal process for receiving potential EIPs and approving them for official endorsement.

One proposal type is an ERC (Ethereum Request for Comments) which proposes a new type of token on the Ethereum blockchain. Tokens that follow that standard become known by their ERC number (ERC-20 tokens).

Without these standard interfaces, every contract could require a different app, with different rules, and non-technical users would get confused or scammed by contracts.

Tokens

Smart contracts allow users to mint, distribute, and sell their own currencies which are called "tokens". The most common contract for tokens is ERC-20, but many later ERCs have added features. If a token is issued compatible with ERC-20, you can store it and make transactions from several wallets. There now are secondary markets and exchanges for tokens.

Unlike cryptocurrencies, which have a supply and distribution plan for miners, you can theoretically create and distribute as many tokens as you want from nothing. Unfortunately, you won't become a trillionaire by creating billions of tokens and selling them for $1,000 each. For buy-in, you should find an application for using and/or selling your tokens - something to make users value them.

Suppose your bakery creates a Crypto Cupcake. As the orders come in, people start asking for gift certificates for their friends, classmates, and coworkers.  You could make a batch of 10,000 Gift-Tokens and sell them at your standard price. As long as your store remains open, the store can accept tokens as payment, and you have cupcakes to satisfy demand, the tokens will retain value.  In this case we don't expect a secondary market of cupcakes, but stranger things have happened.

CoinMetrics reported that there are now more ERC-20 token transactions than Ether currency transactions.

As of November 10th [2019], ERC-20’s had about 303,000 daily transactions vs about 290,000 for ETH.

ICOs

An Initial Coin Offering (ICO) is the cryptocurrency equivalent of an Initial Public Offering (IPO). It's an umbrella term for the first sales of a new cryptocurrency or token. Companies may use favorable pricing or rewards to encourage professional investors, exchanges, and individual users to invest early in the ICO - allowing the company to have cash and cryptocurrency reserves to continue operations.

After a boom in ICOs in 2017, and many projects ending in failures or losses, the number of new ICOs has dwindled. This isn't necessarily a flaw in using an ICO to raise money, but they had become weighed down by several which turned out to be poorly designed, misleading, get-rich-quick schemes, or outright fraud (exit scams).

The US SEC produced a website (HoweyCoins) mimicking the typical layout of an ICO announcement, and warning individuals not to be persuaded. Check out their website to learn more about common marketing techniques to trick investors.

IEOs

An Initial Exchange Offering (IEO) is an evolution of the ICO model. A recent Reuters article explains the distinction:

IEOs take place on cryptocurrency exchanges which, for a fee, effectively act as middlemen, performing functions such as due diligence on a project, “know your customer” screening, marketing and selling tokens to customers.

Howey Test and the SEC

The SEC had other concerns with ICOs. After the DAO project raised funds from crowdfunding investors without oversight, in mid-2017 the SEC issued a report (sometimes called the DAO report) describing the responsibilities of cryptographic tokens and financial securities.

The DAO might be a forerunner of autonomously-run international corporations. Bitcoin and Ethereum currently are accepted as currencies or commodities, but many ICOs and tokens are classified as SECURITIES, which cannot be sold without careful legal compliance and public disclosures. The current standard for determining whether a token is a security is THE DAO REPORT and THE HOWEY TEST. The SEC's decisions are followed closely because even though cryptocurrencies and smart contracts CAN operate in other countries or without complying with regulations, it is exceedingly difficult to appeal to users, use the funds, and pay employees without touching the banking system. If your program has any connection to the non-crypto world (a legal entity, a bank account, a public website, real identities of leaders, directors, or employees) then this is enough to be shut down. Only a few websites can inspire investor or customer confidence with this level of secrecy. Gambling and drug marketplaces have managed it, but ultimately were shut down for breaking other US laws.

A 2019 lawsuit filed by the SEC against Kik Messenger illustrates many issues with launching a non-compliant cryptocurrency with public appeals to American investors.

Social Media Essentials

HODL

A common meme in the cryptocurrency world is 'HODL' (a misspelling of HOLD), the idea being to hold, and not hastily sell, one's Bitcoin when the price rises or falls. The goal of a HODL-er is to wait for their Bitcoin to be much more valuable, or to be accepted everywhere as a new currency. This HODL joke is carried forward, for example 'BUIDL' for Ethereum hackathons.

Bitcoin Maximalists

Some Bitcoin users are convinced that no other national currency, cryptocurrency or fork will rival the original Bitcoin in terms of value, utility, and reliability in the long run. Under that belief, they reject any other cryptocurrency. They discuss problems with other currencies and share lists of Bitcoin-only apps.

These 'Bitcoin maximalists' have a point, as Bitcoin is the oldest and most-mined cryptocurrency available. In a US Congress committee discussion about Facebook and the Libra cryptocurrency, Congressman Warren Davidson mentioned that altcoins were sometimes called "s--tcoins", a term often used by Bitcoin maximalists.

Scams

Many scammers reply to cryptocurrency posts on Twitter and other social networks. They use the profile photo, name, and screenshots to pretend to be the original poster, a cryptocurrency founder or celebrity, or an ordinary user who received money through a drawing or giveaway. In some cases they will hack a verified account or use special Unicode characters to look just like a celebrity (they often impersonate Elon Musk or Ethereum founder Vitalik Buterin).

Never send cryptocurrency to anyone promising a giveaway or multiplier. There is no way to recover sent funds. Take caution with a stranger's recommended currency or token if their deal seems 'too good to be true' or it is pitched as a 'limited time offer'.
